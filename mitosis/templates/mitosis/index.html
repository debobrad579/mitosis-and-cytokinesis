Stages of Mitosis

  • Interphase

    Interphase is the longest phase a cell will go through in its life span; it is the lifecycle of the cell. In this phase, the cell will grow, replicate its DNA, and prepare for mitosis. A cell does its main cell functions such as processing proteins, or producing energy during this phase.

  • Prophase

    Prophase is the first phase of cell division; it's the first stage of mitosis. During prophase, the DNA that the cell replicated at the end of interphase is condensed down to less than half the original lengths. These new condensed chromosomes consist of two sister chromatids joined together at the centromere. The nuclear membrane breaks during this phase.

  • Metaphase

    "Meta," can mean middle; metaphase is the stage of mitosis where the sister chromatids align at the equator (middle) of the cell. Spindle fibers attach to the centromere in this phase.

  • Anaphase

    Anaphase is the phase when the replicated cells are pulled apart by spindle fibers. "Ana," can mean opposite; during anaphase, the replicated sister chromatids move apart towards the opposite poles of the cell.

  • Telophase

    "Telo," can mean end or completion; telophase is the final stage of mitosis. During telophase in animal cells, the cell membranes pinches inwards, and the cytoplasm splits in half. During telophase in plant cells, a new cell wall forms across the equator of the cell.

  • Cytokinesis

    Cytokinesis refers to the actual splitting of the cytoplasm. The two daughter cells return to interphase once they separate. In animal cells, a cleavage furrow forms; in plant cells, a cell plate forms. A cleavage furrow is an indentation in the cell's surface marking the cell's "pinching". A cell plate is the beginning of a new cell wall.
